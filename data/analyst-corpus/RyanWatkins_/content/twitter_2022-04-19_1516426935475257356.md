### Tweet 1/5
**@RyanWatkins_** | 2022-04-19 | [link](https://x.com/RyanWatkins_/status/1516426935475257356)

Building a currency is an unnecessary undertaking for most blockchains (inclusive of L1s, L2s, app-chain, etc).

Many would be better off having their validators accept fees paid in external assets which have more credibility and reliability as currencies (BTC, ETH, stablecoins).

---

### Tweet 2/5
**@RyanWatkins_** | 2022-04-19 | [link](https://x.com/RyanWatkins_/status/1516426933936009216)

The idea of L1s accruing value from fee generation is convoluted by the circular logic of them generating fees in currencies they issue.

The only economic frameworks that make sense for understanding the native assets of L1s are that of commodities or currencies.

---

### Tweet 3/5
**@RyanWatkins_** | 2022-04-19 | [link](https://x.com/RyanWatkins_/status/1516426938704961543)

But if commodities are the best framework for thinking through these native assets, then many blockchains will likely need to undergo tokeneconomic revamps to better calibrate these assets to accrue value (tweaking supply emissions, adding burning mechanisms, etc)

### Tweet 4/5
**@RyanWatkins_** | 2022-04-19 | [link](https://x.com/RyanWatkins_/status/1516426937660493826)

The native assets of smart contract platforms may ultimately be valued this way as well. 

ETH has long been dubbed digital oil for years now for this reason (oil is a much larger market than gold btw).

### Tweet 5/5

The exceptions to this model would blockchains whose native asset’s primary use is neither as a currency or fee payment asset, but more like a commodity (i.e use asset to access resources blockchain is providing).

Helium’s HNT is a good example of this.
